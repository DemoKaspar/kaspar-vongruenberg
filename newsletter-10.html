Newsletter Issue #10

Freedom

Hey {NAME},

Issue #10! Double digits! It feels like just yesterday I was writing about the early conversations around developer productivity. Now we're seeing Platform Engineering become a recognized discipline with conferences, job titles, and dedicated teams at major companies.

Today I want to talk about something philosophical: freedom in platform engineering. Because at its core, that's what we're really trying to achieve - freedom for developers to focus on what matters.

The Freedom Paradox

There's a fascinating paradox in platform engineering: to give developers more freedom, you often have to constrain their choices. To make them more productive, you have to impose standards and abstractions.

This creates tension. Developers want autonomy - the ability to choose their own tools, make their own architecture decisions, and solve problems their way. But they also want velocity - the ability to ship code quickly without getting bogged down in infrastructure complexity.

The best platforms I've seen resolve this tension by being opinionated about infrastructure concerns while remaining flexible about application concerns. They provide strong defaults but clear escape hatches. They standardize the boring stuff so developers can focus on the interesting stuff.

Different Types of Freedom

Not all freedom is created equal. In my experience, there are roughly three types of freedom that developers care about:

1. Freedom FROM complexity
This is about abstracting away infrastructure concerns so developers don't have to think about them. Freedom from managing Kubernetes manifests, from configuring CI/CD pipelines, from debugging networking issues.

2. Freedom TO experiment
This is about making it easy to try new things - spin up new environments, test different architectures, prototype new features. The platform should make experimentation cheap and fast.

3. Freedom WITHIN constraints
This is about providing flexibility within well-defined boundaries. Developers can choose their framework, their database, their messaging patterns - but within a set of supported options that the platform knows how to handle.

The Golden Path Philosophy

One concept that's really resonated with teams is the idea of "Golden Paths" - well-lit, well-maintained paths that make it easy to do common things correctly.

A golden path isn't a mandate - it's a recommendation backed by great tooling and documentation. It's the path of least resistance for solving common problems, but it's not the only path.

The key is making the golden path so much easier than alternatives that developers choose it voluntarily. When they do need to deviate, the platform should support that too - but with the understanding that they're taking on additional complexity.

Self-Service as Liberation

The most liberating thing a platform can provide is self-service capabilities. When developers can provision what they need, when they need it, without tickets or approvals or waiting for ops teams - that's when you see productivity really take off.

But self-service isn't just about speed - it's about ownership. When developers can manage their own environments, they develop better intuition about operational concerns. They make better architectural decisions because they understand the operational implications.

The goal isn't to shield developers from operations entirely - it's to give them operational capabilities that are appropriate to their skill level and role.

Measuring Freedom

How do you know if your platform is providing the right kind of freedom? Here are some metrics I look for:

Adoption rate: Are developers choosing to use the platform, or are they being forced to? High voluntary adoption usually indicates that the platform is providing valuable freedom.

Escape hatch usage: How often do developers need to work around the platform? Some usage is expected and healthy, but high usage might indicate the platform is too restrictive.

Time to value: How long does it take for a developer to go from idea to running code? This measures freedom from complexity.

Experimentation frequency: How often are teams trying new approaches, spinning up test environments, prototyping new features? This measures freedom to experiment.

The Road to Platform Engineering

Looking back at the journey from the early days of DevOps to where we are now with Platform Engineering, I think what's changed is our understanding of how to balance freedom and constraint.

Early DevOps was often about giving developers more operational responsibility - "You build it, you run it." But we learned that not every developer wants or should have deep operational expertise.

Platform Engineering is about finding the right abstraction layer - giving developers enough operational capability to be effective while shielding them from complexity that doesn't serve their primary purpose.

It's about freedom through structure, not freedom despite structure.

What We're Learning

At Humanitec, we're constantly learning about how to provide the right kind of freedom. Some insights from recent conversations with customers:

Developers want to understand what's happening under the hood, even if they don't want to manage it directly. Transparency is crucial for trust.

The best abstractions feel inevitable once you understand them, but non-obvious before you do. Finding the right abstraction layer is more art than science.

Different teams need different levels of freedom. Your platform needs to accommodate both teams that want maximum abstraction and teams that need more control.

What does freedom mean to your development teams? How are you balancing constraint and flexibility in your platform?

Kaspar
