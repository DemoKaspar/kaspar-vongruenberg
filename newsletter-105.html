Design principles to get your platform ready for agents // + big announcements on Plat2026!

Hey {NAME},

I spent last week building a vibe coding setup on GCP (here is a video I recorded on the final result). Last week I also had a larger workshop with AWS in London and I summarized what really stood out to me when modelling a platform in a way it can not only be used by humans but also by AI agents. That turned out to be an interesting architectural challenge and I thought I'd summarize my learnings on how to architect platforms in an AI first world. Before we start, let's just clarify that I find the word agent as blurry as you. I really mean any type of inferring AI/LLM/Agent. So, let us start:

Design for a 20 Year Old Intern

What? Yes! If your platform, your interfaces, how one consumes your path etc. isn't so self-explanatory that a 20 year old intern can pick this up with zero training, an agent cannot either. This means:

  • Are the conceptual models and the language you are using easy to understand and logic? And this includes everything from capabilities over API and interface design naming conventions etc.
  • Is everything documented? Nothing will work without excellent documentation. Somebody in the audience said that will be a problem because they only have things in closed PDFs and what to do now. To which my simple answer is: change the org before it goes out of business…

Define Your Line of Determinism

Delicious term, right? Well what I mean is more straight forward: you need to decide for yourself how far you feel comfortable an agent takes non deterministic decisions. LLMs estimate the next most likely word, they do not care about your policy conventions. So you probably don't want them to guess the configuration of your RDS database in production by vibe coding some Terraform. You are however probably ok to have an agent express in general "I need a postgres database for production" and then hand this over to a deterministic system that fires up that DB with exactly the policy configs your org requires. That hand over point between how far the agent can go until deterministic APIs take over is what I call the line of determinism. In order for that line to be a smooth and productive experience:

  • You need to focus on your backend and structure it well (again conceptual model relevant here!)
  • The interfaces need to be easy to consume safely (RBAC for agents is a thing!)
  • Limit choice - as a rule of thumb in order to get stable outcomes you want systems trained on large amounts of data (LLMS) to make decisions against a very limited number of options.
  • Think about maintainability from the get go. Your 500 person marketing team vibe coding databases and nobody cleans up etc. will become a disaster.
  • Ensure inter-system balance of power. Which is a fancy way of saying let different parts of your toolchain mistrust each other. If a model makes a choice, maybe have a second model doubt it. If the choice is made, run tests. Before you deploy let OPA run a policy check etc. These things matter!
