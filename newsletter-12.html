Newsletter Issue #12

Maturity

Hey {NAME},

Issue #12, and today I want to talk about something that doesn't get enough attention in platform engineering: organizational maturity and how it should shape your platform strategy.

I keep seeing teams try to implement the same platform patterns regardless of their organization's size, maturity, or constraints. But the platform that works for Netflix won't work for a 50-person startup, and the approach that makes sense for a greenfield project won't work for a 20-year-old enterprise.

Maturity matters, and your platform should reflect where your organization actually is, not where you wish it were.

The Maturity Stages

Based on conversations with hundreds of engineering teams, I've identified four distinct maturity stages that shape platform requirements:

Stage 1: Startup (5-50 developers)
Everyone does everything. No dedicated operations team. Focus is on shipping features and finding product-market fit. Infrastructure decisions are made by whoever is available.

Stage 2: Scale-up (50-200 developers)
First dedicated operations/infrastructure roles. Growing pains around deployment consistency and operational overhead. Need for standardization becomes apparent.

Stage 3: Enterprise (200-1000 developers)
Multiple product teams, dedicated platform/SRE teams. Complex regulatory and compliance requirements. Need for sophisticated abstractions and governance.

Stage 4: Large Enterprise (1000+ developers)
Multiple business units, acquisitions, legacy systems. Diverse technology stacks and organizational patterns. Platform becomes a competitive advantage.

Stage 1: Startup Platform Needs

At the startup stage, your platform needs are simple but critical:

Core requirements:

  • Fast deployment pipeline (minutes, not hours)
  • Basic monitoring and alerting
  • Simple database and cache provisioning
  • Cost visibility and control

What NOT to build:

  • Complex multi-tenancy systems
  • Sophisticated RBAC and governance
  • Custom orchestration platforms
  • Comprehensive service catalogs

Use managed services for everything you can. Your platform should be held together with scripts, not custom software. The goal is enabling speed and learning, not operational excellence.

Stage 2: Scale-up Platform Evolution

As you grow, new problems emerge that require platform thinking:

New requirements:

  • Deployment standardization across teams
  • Environment management and promotion workflows
  • Basic security scanning and compliance
  • Resource limits and cost allocation
  • On-call rotation and incident management

This is where you start building your first real platform components - standardized deployment pipelines, environment provisioning tools, and basic developer self-service capabilities.

The key insight: focus on reducing operational overhead, not building abstractions. You want to eliminate toil and manual processes before you start optimizing for developer experience.

Stage 3: Enterprise Platform Sophistication

At enterprise scale, platform engineering becomes a real discipline:

Advanced requirements:

  • Multi-team governance and policy enforcement
  • Sophisticated CI/CD workflows with approval gates
  • Service mesh and advanced networking
  • Comprehensive observability and SLO management
  • Security scanning, vulnerability management, compliance automation
  • Cost optimization and resource management

This is where you invest in platform teams that think like product teams. You need user research, roadmaps, and metrics. You start building custom tooling and abstractions that are tailored to your organization's specific needs.

Stage 4: Large Enterprise Platform Strategy

At large enterprise scale, platforms become strategic capabilities:

Strategic requirements:

  • Multi-cloud and hybrid cloud support
  • Legacy system integration and modernization
  • Business unit autonomy with central governance
  • Acquisition integration and technology diversity
  • Regulatory compliance across multiple jurisdictions
  • Platform as competitive differentiator

At this stage, you might build or buy sophisticated platform orchestration tools. You have dedicated platform product managers, user research, and platform marketing. Your platform becomes a moat that enables faster innovation than competitors.

Common Maturity Mistakes

Here are the most common mistakes I see teams make when they don't align their platform strategy with their maturity:

Premature optimization: Stage 1 companies trying to build Stage 3 platforms. You don't need service mesh when you have 3 services.

Maturity lag: Stage 3 companies still using Stage 1 approaches. When you have 100 developers, scripts and manual processes don't scale.

Over-engineering: Building sophisticated abstractions before understanding the underlying patterns and requirements.

Under-investing: Not recognizing when you've reached a stage where platform investment becomes essential for continued growth.

Technology Choices by Maturity

Different maturity stages call for different technology approaches:

Stage 1: Managed services, simple CI/CD (GitHub Actions), basic monitoring (Datadog), minimal infrastructure (Heroku, AWS Fargate)

Stage 2: Containerization (Docker), basic orchestration (ECS, Cloud Run), infrastructure as code (Terraform), structured logging

Stage 3: Kubernetes, GitOps (ArgoCD), service mesh (Istio), comprehensive observability (Prometheus + Grafana), policy engines

Stage 4: Multi-cloud orchestration, custom platform tooling, sophisticated policy and governance systems, platform as code

Measuring Platform Maturity

How do you know which stage you're in? Look at these indicators:

Team structure: Do you have dedicated platform/SRE teams? How much of their time is spent on custom tooling vs. managing existing systems?

Developer experience: How long does it take to onboard a new developer? How much infrastructure knowledge do they need?

Operational complexity: How many manual processes exist in your deployment pipeline? How often do deployments fail due to environmental issues?

Standardization level: How consistent are deployments across teams? How much configuration drift exists between environments?

Evolution, Not Revolution

The key insight: platform evolution should be gradual and driven by real pain points, not theoretical benefits.

Don't skip stages. A Stage 1 company should focus on getting to Stage 2 successfully before thinking about Stage 3 problems. Trying to jump stages usually results in over-engineered solutions that don't solve actual problems.

But also don't get stuck in old patterns. When you reach a new maturity stage, the approaches that got you there won't get you to the next stage.

Questions for Reflection

As you think about your platform strategy, consider:

  • What stage is your organization actually in, not where you want to be?
  • Are your platform investments aligned with your current maturity level?
  • What are the specific pain points you're trying to solve today?
  • What capabilities will you need as you grow to the next stage?
  • Are you building for today's problems or tomorrow's problems?

Platform engineering is ultimately about solving the problems you actually have, not the problems you think you should have.

What stage is your organization in? How has your platform approach evolved as you've grown?
