Newsletter Issue #15

How to () Build Internal Tooling for Developers

Hey {NAME},

Issue #15! We're hitting our stride here. Today I want to dive deep into something that every platform team struggles with: how to build internal tooling that developers actually want to use.

I've seen too many well-intentioned platform teams build amazing technical solutions that sit unused because they didn't think about the human side of the equation.

The Internal Tool Paradox

Here's the challenge: developers are your most demanding users. They're technical, opinionated, and they have alternatives (building their own solutions, using external tools, or just ignoring your platform entirely).

But they're also your most valuable users. When developers love your internal tooling, they become your biggest advocates. They contribute improvements, they help onboard other teams, and they push the boundaries of what's possible.

The difference between success and failure often comes down to understanding that building internal tooling is really product management for an audience of one: developers.

Start with Jobs to Be Done

Before you write a single line of code, understand what job developers are hiring your tool to do. And I don't mean the obvious functional job - dig deeper into the emotional and social jobs.

For example:

Functional job: "Deploy my application to production"

Emotional job: "Feel confident that my deployment won't break things"

Social job: "Look competent to my team and avoid the blame when things go wrong"

The best internal tools address all three dimensions. They not only solve the technical problem, but they also reduce anxiety and protect the developer's reputation.

Design for the Daily Workflow

Most internal tools are designed for the happy path - the ideal scenario where everything works perfectly. But developers spend most of their time dealing with edge cases, debugging problems, and working around limitations.

Design your tools for these messy, real-world scenarios:

  • When deployments fail: Make it easy to understand what went wrong and how to fix it
  • When services are down: Provide clear status information and escalation paths
  • When requirements change: Make it easy to modify configurations and rollback changes
  • When developers are learning: Provide discovery mechanisms and progressive disclosure

The CLI vs. GUI Question

One of the biggest decisions you'll make is whether to build CLI tools, web interfaces, or both. Here's what I've learned:

CLIs are for power users and automation. They're perfect for repetitive tasks, scripting, and developers who live in the terminal. They're also essential for CI/CD integration.

GUIs are for discoverability and complex workflows. They're great for exploring capabilities, visualizing system state, and tasks that involve lots of related information.

The best internal tools provide both, with the GUI and CLI offering the same underlying capabilities. Developers should be able to start with the GUI to learn, then graduate to the CLI for efficiency.

Make it Debuggable

This is probably the most important principle for internal tooling: when your tool doesn't work, developers need to be able to figure out why.

This means:

  • Verbose error messages that explain not just what went wrong, but why and how to fix it
  • Logging and tracing that developers can access to understand what the tool is doing
  • Dry-run modes that let developers see what would happen without actually doing it
  • Status endpoints that show the current state of the system
  • Documentation that includes troubleshooting guides and common error scenarios

Remember: when your internal tool breaks, it's blocking developer productivity. The faster they can debug and work around issues, the less frustrated they'll be.

Build for Adoption, Not Perfection

Perfect is the enemy of adopted. It's better to ship something that solves 80% of use cases really well than to spend months building something that handles every possible edge case.

Here's a pattern I've seen work well:

  1. Start with one team's pain point - build something that solves a specific problem for a specific team
  2. Get that team to love it - work closely with them to refine the solution until they can't imagine working without it
  3. Generalize gradually - identify what needs to change to work for similar teams
  4. Scale through champions - have successful teams help evangelize to other teams

Documentation as Product

For internal tooling, documentation isn't an afterthought - it's a core part of the product. Developers won't use tools they can't understand, and they won't contribute to tools they can't modify.

Great internal tool documentation includes:

  • Quick start guides that get developers from zero to productive in minutes
  • Examples and recipes for common use cases
  • Architecture documentation for developers who want to understand how it works
  • Contributing guides for developers who want to help improve the tool
  • Migration guides for moving from existing solutions

Embrace the 80/20 Rule

Not every developer on your team needs to use your internal tooling in the same way. In fact, trying to serve every use case equally well often results in tools that serve no use case particularly well.

Design for the 80% use case, but provide escape hatches for the 20%. This might mean:

  • Opinionated defaults that work for most teams out of the box
  • Configuration options for teams that need customization
  • Plugin systems for teams that need to extend functionality
  • Raw API access for teams that need to build their own interfaces

Measure What Matters

How do you know if your internal tooling is successful? The metrics that matter for internal tools are different from external products:

Adoption rate: What percentage of eligible teams/developers are using the tool?

Frequency of use: Are developers using it once or making it part of their daily workflow?

Time to value: How long does it take for a new developer to get value from the tool?

Support ticket volume: Are developers able to self-serve, or do they need constant help?

Contribution rate: Are developers contributing improvements, bug fixes, or extensions?

The Long Game

Building successful internal tooling is a long-term play. It requires sustained investment, continuous iteration, and a deep understanding of developer needs.

But when you get it right, the leverage is incredible. Great internal tooling doesn't just improve efficiency - it changes how teams work, what they're capable of building, and how they think about their relationship with the platform.

The best internal tools become so essential that teams can't imagine working without them. That's the goal: not just adoption, but dependence born out of genuine value.

What's your experience with building internal tooling? What has worked well for your team? What would you do differently?

Kaspar
