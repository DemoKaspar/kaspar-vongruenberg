Newsletter Issue #20

Service Catalogs, IDPs, and other Buzzwords

Hey {NAME},

Issue #20! We've hit another milestone. Today I want to cut through some of the buzzword confusion that's plaguing our industry and talk about what these terms actually mean in practice.

Everywhere I look, I see people throwing around terms like "Internal Developer Platforms," "Service Catalogs," "Platform Engineering," and "Developer Experience" without much clarity about what they actually mean or how they relate to each other.

Let's fix that.

Internal Developer Platform (IDP)

An Internal Developer Platform is the sum total of all the tools and services that enable software delivery in your organization. It's not a single product - it's an integrated ecosystem.

Think of it like this: if you're a developer who wants to ship code to production, what do you interact with? Your CI/CD system, your deployment tools, your monitoring, your secrets management, your database provisioning, your environment management. All of that together is your IDP.

The key insight: most organizations already have an IDP. It might be a collection of disparate tools held together with scripts and tribal knowledge, but it exists. The question is whether it's designed intentionally or evolved organically.

Good IDPs: Provide consistent interfaces, reduce cognitive load, and get out of the developer's way

Bad IDPs: Require deep infrastructure knowledge, have inconsistent interfaces, and create bottlenecks

Service Catalog

A service catalog is a menu of pre-configured services that developers can provision on-demand. Think "click a button to get a PostgreSQL database" or "select from these application templates."

Service catalogs are often a component of an IDP, but they're not the same thing. You can have an effective IDP without a service catalog, and you can have a service catalog that doesn't improve the overall developer experience.

The value of service catalogs comes from standardization and self-service. Instead of having 47 different ways to deploy a database, you have 3-5 well-tested configurations that cover most use cases.

Service catalogs work when: They provide sensible defaults, clear documentation, and escape hatches for advanced use cases

Service catalogs fail when: They're too restrictive, poorly documented, or don't match actual developer needs

Platform Engineering

Platform Engineering is the discipline of designing and building Internal Developer Platforms. It's about applying product management principles to internal infrastructure.

Platform engineers think about:

  • Developer experience and user journeys
  • Adoption metrics and feedback loops
  • Abstraction layers and interface design
  • Operational excellence and reliability
  • Organizational change and culture

Platform Engineering is different from traditional infrastructure work because it focuses on enabling other teams rather than just keeping systems running.

Developer Experience (DevEx)

Developer Experience is about how it feels to be a developer in your organization. It's the sum total of all interactions developers have with tools, processes, and systems.

DevEx is broader than just technical tooling - it includes:

  • How easy it is to get started on a new project
  • How quickly you can get feedback on changes
  • How much cognitive overhead is required for common tasks
  • How well documentation and support systems work
  • How much friction exists in development workflows

Good DevEx is the outcome of good platform engineering, but it's measured from the developer's perspective, not the platform team's perspective.

How They Fit Together

Here's how I think about the relationships:

Platform Engineering is the discipline

Internal Developer Platforms are what platform engineers build

Service Catalogs are often components within IDPs

Developer Experience is the outcome that matters

You practice Platform Engineering to build an Internal Developer Platform that may include Service Catalogs, all in service of improving Developer Experience.

The Buzzword Problem

Why does this terminology confusion matter? Because it leads to bad decisions.

I've seen teams that think they need to buy a "service catalog" product when what they really need is to simplify their deployment process. I've seen teams that think they're doing "platform engineering" when they're really just managing infrastructure in the traditional way.

The tools and products matter less than the outcomes. You could build an excellent IDP with open source tools, or you could buy expensive platform products and still have terrible developer experience.

What Actually Matters

Instead of getting caught up in terminology, focus on these questions:

For your developers:

  • How long does it take to go from idea to running in production?
  • How much time do you spend on operational concerns vs. feature development?
  • How confident are you that your changes won't break production?
  • How easy is it to debug problems when they occur?

For your organization:

  • How quickly can you onboard new developers?
  • How much duplicated effort exists across teams?
  • How consistent are your operational practices?
  • How well do you handle compliance and security requirements?

Implementation Patterns

Here are some patterns I see working well in practice:

Start with workflows, not tools: Map out the actual steps developers take to deploy code, then identify the friction points. Tools come later.

Build for the 80% case: Your IDP should make common tasks trivially easy, even if it makes edge cases slightly harder.

Provide progressive disclosure: Simple interfaces for simple tasks, but don't hide the underlying complexity entirely. Developers need escape hatches.

Measure developer sentiment: Survey developers regularly about their experience. The platform team's opinion of their platform is less important than the developers' opinion.

Anti-Patterns

And some patterns that consistently fail:

Building platforms for platform teams: Optimizing for operational efficiency instead of developer experience

Abstracting too early: Creating complex abstractions before understanding the underlying patterns and requirements

Ignoring organizational factors: Building great tools that nobody adopts because you didn't think about change management

Copying instead of adapting: Taking another organization's platform approach without adapting it to your specific context

The Real Test

Here's how you know if your platform engineering efforts are working: developers start asking for more platform capabilities instead of fewer.

When you've built something genuinely useful, developers will push the boundaries and ask for extensions. They'll contribute improvements and suggest new features. They'll evangelize the platform to other teams.

If developers are trying to work around your platform instead of with it, you've probably optimized for the wrong thing.

Looking Forward

I think we're still in the early stages of figuring out platform engineering as a discipline. The tools are getting better, the patterns are becoming clearer, and the organizational understanding is improving.

But we need to stay focused on outcomes rather than getting caught up in the latest buzzwords or products. The goal is developer productivity, not platform sophistication.

What's your experience with these concepts? Are you seeing clarity or confusion in how your organization talks about platforms and developer experience?

Kaspar
