Newsletter Issue #25

Butterflies & Compensation

Hey {NAME},

Quarter of a century of these newsletters! Time flies when you're having fun talking about platform engineering and developer productivity.

Today I want to talk about something that doesn't get discussed enough in our community: the butterfly effect of platform decisions and how compensation models affect platform adoption. These might seem like unrelated topics, but bear with me - there's a connection.

The Platform Butterfly Effect

Every platform engineering decision has cascading effects that are hard to predict. A seemingly minor change to your deployment pipeline can ripple through your organization in unexpected ways.

I was talking to a platform team last week who made what they thought was a simple change - they standardized on a new secrets management approach. They expected some minor grumbling from developers about having to learn a new tool. What they didn't expect was how it would affect their hiring process.

Suddenly, they could hire developers from a much broader talent pool because the new secrets management system was industry-standard, whereas their old homegrown solution required extensive onboarding. The time savings in hiring and onboarding more than paid for the migration effort.

This is the butterfly effect in action - small platform changes can have disproportionate impacts on business outcomes that are hard to anticipate.

The Compensation Conundrum

Here's something interesting: the way you compensate and promote engineers has a huge impact on platform adoption success.

If your promotion criteria reward building new features and shipping code, but not adopting platform tools or contributing to developer experience, guess what happens? Developers optimize for what gets rewarded, not what's best for the organization.

I've seen platform teams build amazing tools that sit unused because using them doesn't advance anyone's career. Meanwhile, developers get rewarded for building custom solutions that duplicate platform functionality because "building" is visible in a way that "adopting" isn't.

Compensation patterns that hurt platform adoption:

  • Rewarding lines of code or commits
  • Promoting based only on feature delivery
  • Measuring individual productivity in isolation
  • Ignoring operational excellence in performance reviews

Compensation patterns that help platform adoption:

  • Rewarding team productivity improvements
  • Recognizing operational excellence and reliability
  • Including platform adoption in performance metrics
  • Celebrating developers who help others be more productive

Aligning Incentives

The most successful platform organizations I've studied have figured out how to align individual incentives with platform goals.

Some examples of what this looks like in practice:

Team-based metrics: Instead of measuring individual developer productivity, measure team delivery speed and reliability. This encourages collaboration and platform adoption.

Platform contribution recognition: Explicitly recognize developers who contribute to platform tools, write good documentation, or help onboard others to platform practices.

Operational ownership: Tie compensation to the operational health of the services developers build, not just their initial delivery. This creates incentives to use platform tools that improve reliability.

Cross-functional impact: Reward developers who make platform improvements that benefit other teams, even if it doesn't directly impact their own feature delivery.

The Network Effects

Platform adoption has strong network effects - the value increases exponentially as more teams adopt the platform. But these network effects only kick in once you reach critical mass.

This creates a chicken-and-egg problem: early adopters don't see much benefit because the network is small, so it's hard to get to critical mass. But if you can get to critical mass, the benefits become obvious and adoption accelerates.

Your compensation and incentive systems play a crucial role in getting over this adoption hump. You need to artificially incentivize early adoption until the natural network effects take over.

Measuring the Right Things

If you want platform adoption, you need to measure and reward things that drive platform adoption:

Team velocity metrics: How fast can teams go from idea to production? This naturally favors platform usage.

Reliability metrics: What's the operational health of services? Platforms typically improve reliability.

Onboarding speed: How fast can new team members become productive? Good platforms dramatically reduce onboarding time.

Standard compliance: How well do teams follow organizational standards? Platforms make compliance easier.

Knowledge sharing: How much do teams help each other? Platform adoption often requires cultural change.

The Long Game

Building a successful platform is a long game that requires sustained organizational commitment. But most performance review cycles are quarterly or annual, which creates a mismatch in time horizons.

You need to find ways to create short-term rewards for behaviors that have long-term platform benefits. This might mean explicitly rewarding platform adoption in the early stages, even if it temporarily slows feature delivery.

The butterfly effect works in your favor here - small investments in aligning incentives can have disproportionate impacts on platform success.

Questions for Reflection

As you think about your platform strategy, consider:

  • What behaviors are your promotion and compensation systems actually rewarding?
  • Do individual incentives align with platform adoption goals?
  • How are you measuring and rewarding team-level productivity improvements?
  • What unexpected butterfly effects have you seen from platform changes?

Platform engineering isn't just about technology - it's about creating the organizational conditions for technology to succeed.

What have you seen work (or not work) in terms of incentivizing platform adoption?

Kaspar
