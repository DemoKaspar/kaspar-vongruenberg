Newsletter Issue #30

In the best teams

Hey {NAME},

Issue #30! That's a nice round number. It feels like a good time to reflect on what I've learned from observing some of the best platform engineering teams out there.

Over the past year, I've had the privilege of working with and learning from dozens of platform teams - from 10-person startups to Fortune 500 enterprises. The best teams share some fascinating common traits that aren't always obvious.

They Obsess Over Developer Experience

The best platform teams don't just build infrastructure - they design experiences. They think deeply about every touchpoint a developer has with the platform.

They ask questions like:

  • What's the first impression when someone tries to deploy their first application?
  • How does it feel to debug a problem at 2 AM?
  • What's the emotional journey of onboarding to the platform?
  • How can we reduce cognitive load at every step?

I've seen platform teams that have UX designers. I've seen platform teams that do user research with developers. I've seen platform teams that A/B test their documentation.

The technology is just the implementation detail. The experience is the product.

They Measure What Matters

The best teams have moved beyond vanity metrics like "number of deployments" or "platform adoption rate." They measure outcomes that actually correlate with business value:

Time to value for new services: How long from the first line of code to serving production traffic?

Mean time to recovery: When things break (and they will), how quickly can we get back to normal?

Developer cognitive load: How much mental effort does it take to use the platform effectively?

Operational burden reduction: How much manual work have we eliminated for both developers and ops teams?

But here's the key insight: they don't just measure these things - they have dashboards that show trends over time, and they make platform investment decisions based on these metrics.

They Embrace Boring Technology

This might be the most counterintuitive trait: the best platform teams are conservative about technology choices.

They use Postgres instead of the latest NoSQL database. They use Kubernetes instead of the newest container orchestrator. They use proven monitoring tools instead of the hot new observability startup.

Why? Because platforms are infrastructure, and infrastructure should be boring. The innovation should happen in the applications running on the platform, not in the platform itself.

The teams that chase the latest technology trends end up spending all their time fighting infrastructure fires instead of enabling developers.

They Build Communities, Not Just Tools

The best platform teams understand that adoption is as much about culture and community as it is about technology.

They have:

  • Regular office hours where developers can get help
  • Internal documentation that rivals external open source projects
  • Champions programs that turn power users into advocates
  • Feedback loops that make developers feel heard
  • Migration support that holds teams' hands through transitions

They treat internal developer adoption like a B2B SaaS company treats customer success.

They Start Small and Scale Incrementally

The best platform teams resist the urge to boil the ocean. They start by solving one specific problem for one specific team really well.

Then they gradually expand - solving the same problem for more teams, then solving adjacent problems for the same teams.

I've seen teams that started with just a standardized CI/CD pipeline for one product team, then expanded to deployment automation, then environment management, then observability.

Each step proved value before moving to the next. Each step built confidence and political capital for the next expansion.

They Have Strong Product Management

This is a big one: the best platform teams have dedicated product managers, or engineers who think like product managers.

They maintain roadmaps based on user research. They prioritize features based on impact and effort. They communicate platform vision and strategy across the organization.

Platform engineering is product management for internal tools. The best teams embrace this reality instead of fighting it.

They Design for Day 2 Operations

Anyone can build a platform that works on day one. The best teams build platforms that are still working - and still improving - on day 1,000.

This means:

  • Designing for upgrades and migrations from the beginning
  • Building comprehensive monitoring and observability
  • Planning for scale from the start
  • Creating runbooks and operational documentation
  • Designing failure modes that are safe and debuggable

Day 2 operations is where most platforms fail. The best teams design for this from day one.

They Balance Abstraction and Escape Hatches

The best platforms provide sensible defaults and smooth paths for common use cases, but they also provide escape hatches for edge cases.

They understand that the goal isn't to hide all complexity forever - it's to hide complexity until it becomes relevant.

When developers need to drop down to lower levels of abstraction, the platform makes it possible without breaking the higher-level abstractions.

They Invest in Internal Marketing

Build it and they will come works for baseball fields, not for internal platforms.

The best teams have launch strategies, communication plans, and ongoing marketing efforts. They celebrate wins publicly, share success stories, and make platform usage visible and rewarded.

They understand that changing how engineers work is fundamentally a change management challenge, not just a technical challenge.

Common Anti-Patterns

I've also noticed some common patterns in teams that struggle:

They solve problems developers don't have: Building comprehensive solutions for theoretical problems instead of listening to actual pain points.

They optimize for platform team efficiency instead of developer experience: Making decisions that make the platform easier to maintain but harder to use.

They abstract too early: Creating complex abstractions before understanding the underlying patterns and requirements.

They ignore the social side: Focusing entirely on technology and ignoring the human and organizational factors that determine adoption.

The Pattern

Here's what I've noticed: the best platform teams treat platform engineering as applied product management. They're building products for an internal market, and they apply all the same disciplines you'd use for external products.

User research. Product-market fit. Metrics-driven iteration. Go-to-market strategy. Customer success.

The technology is just the implementation. The real work is understanding users, solving real problems, and driving adoption.

What patterns have you seen in successful platform teams? What separates the great ones from the merely good ones?

Kaspar
