Newsletter Issue #35

Thank you

Hey {NAME},

Issue #35, and I wanted to take a moment to say thank you.

When I started this newsletter, I honestly wasn't sure anyone would read it. Platform engineering felt like a niche topic that maybe a few dozen people cared about. I was writing into the void, hoping to connect with other practitioners who were wrestling with similar challenges.

But here we are, 35 issues later, and this newsletter now reaches tens of thousands of platform engineers, developers, and engineering leaders around the world. More importantly, it's sparked countless conversations, connections, and collaborations that are pushing the entire discipline forward.

What We've Built Together

This newsletter has become more than I ever imagined it could be. It's become a focal point for a community of people who believe that we can do better - that we can build tools and systems that make developers' lives better, that we can solve the complexity problems that plague modern software development.

Some highlights from this year:

The conversations: The responses I get to these newsletters are incredible. Detailed technical discussions, war stories from the trenches, challenges and solutions I never would have thought of. You've made this a two-way conversation instead of a monologue.

The community: Platform engineering is no longer a niche practice. It's a recognized discipline with job titles, conference tracks, and dedicated teams at major companies. We've helped legitimize and define this space.

The impact: I've heard from teams who've changed their platform strategies based on insights from this newsletter. I've seen connections formed between practitioners that have led to better solutions and shared learnings.

What I've Learned

Writing this newsletter has taught me as much as I hope it's taught readers. Some key insights that have emerged over 35 issues:

Platform engineering is fundamentally about people, not technology. The technical challenges are real, but the hardest problems are organizational - adoption, change management, building trust, aligning incentives.

Context matters more than best practices. There are patterns that work across organizations, but the specific implementation always depends on company size, culture, technical constraints, and business requirements.

Developer experience is measurable and improvable. It's not just a touchy-feely concept - there are concrete metrics that correlate with business outcomes, and specific interventions that reliably improve those metrics.

The platform engineering community is incredibly generous. People share their failures as openly as their successes. They contribute to open source projects, write detailed case studies, and help each other solve problems.

The Challenges Ahead

As platform engineering has grown, new challenges have emerged:

The complexity trap: As platforms become more sophisticated, they risk becoming the thing they were meant to solve - a source of complexity instead of an abstraction layer that hides complexity.

The scaling challenge: Patterns that work for 50 developers don't always work for 500 or 5,000. We're still learning how to build platforms that scale both technically and organizationally.

The skills gap: Platform engineering requires a unique combination of technical depth, product thinking, and organizational awareness. These skills are rare, and we need to get better at developing them.

The vendor landscape: As the market has matured, we've seen an explosion of tools and products claiming to solve platform engineering problems. Separating signal from noise is getting harder.

What's Next

Looking forward, I'm excited about several trends:

Standards and interoperability: We're starting to see common interfaces and standards emerge that make it easier to build composable platform solutions. Score is one example, but I expect to see more.

AI-assisted platform engineering: AI tools are already helping with code generation and debugging. I think we'll see them become increasingly useful for platform operations, optimization, and even design.

Platform engineering as a service: Not every organization needs to build their own platform from scratch. We're seeing more options for platform-as-a-service that provide the benefits of platform engineering without the implementation overhead.

Measurement and benchmarking: The industry is getting better at measuring developer productivity and platform effectiveness. This will help teams make better decisions and justify investments.

The Community

One of the most rewarding aspects of this newsletter has been watching the platform engineering community grow and mature. We've moved from a small group of practitioners sharing war stories to a real discipline with:

  • Conferences and meetups (PlatformCon is now a major event!)
  • Academic research on developer productivity and platform effectiveness
  • Training programs and certification paths
  • Industry reports and benchmarking studies
  • Professional networks and communities

This community has been incredibly welcoming to newcomers while maintaining high standards for rigor and authenticity. It's a community I'm proud to be part of.

Thank You

So, thank you. Thank you for reading, for responding, for sharing your experiences, for challenging my assumptions, and for building this community together.

Thank you to the platform engineers who are in the trenches every day, building tools that make other developers' lives better. Your work often goes unrecognized, but it's essential to everything we build in software.

Thank you to the engineering leaders who have invested in platform engineering, even when the ROI wasn't immediately obvious. Your support has made this discipline possible.

Thank you to the developers who have been patient with platform teams as we've learned what works and what doesn't. Your feedback has made platforms better.

And thank you to everyone who has contributed to the broader conversation - through blog posts, conference talks, open source contributions, and countless discussions on Slack channels and forums.

Looking Forward

We're just getting started. Platform engineering is still a young discipline, and there's so much more to figure out. How do we build platforms that scale to tens of thousands of developers? How do we make platform engineering accessible to smaller organizations? How do we measure and optimize developer experience at scale?

I don't have all the answers, but I'm excited to keep exploring these questions with you.

Here's to the next 35 issues and beyond. Thank you for being part of this journey.

With gratitude,
Kaspar
