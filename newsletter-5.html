Newsletter Issue #5

Developer Productivity & Experience - Religions

Hi Eric,

Issue #5, and today I want to talk about something that's been bothering me: the religious wars in developer tooling and how they're hindering real progress in developer productivity.

The DevOps Holy Wars

You know what I'm talking about. Kubernetes vs. serverless. Microservices vs. monoliths. GitOps vs. traditional CI/CD. Docker vs. containerd. The list goes on.

These debates consume enormous amounts of energy in our industry, and I think we're missing the point entirely. We're arguing about implementation details when we should be focusing on outcomes.

Here's what I've observed: the teams that are most productive aren't the ones using the "right" technology. They're the teams that have optimized for developer experience within whatever technology constraints they have.

The Productivity Paradox

I've seen teams using "legacy" stacks (monoliths, VMs, manual deployments) that are more productive than teams using cutting-edge technology (microservices, Kubernetes, fully automated pipelines) because they've invested in making their development workflow smooth and predictable.

Meanwhile, I've seen teams spend months migrating to the latest and greatest technology stack, only to find that their developer productivity actually decreased because they didn't think about how the new technology would affect daily development workflows.

The productivity paradox: technology adoption that doesn't improve developer experience often makes things worse, not better.

What Actually Matters

After talking to hundreds of engineering teams, here's what actually correlates with high developer productivity:

Fast feedback loops: Can developers test their changes quickly? Do they get immediate feedback when something breaks?

Reliable infrastructure: Does the development environment work consistently? Do deployments succeed predictably?

Clear ownership: Do developers know who to ask for help? Are responsibilities and interfaces clearly defined?

Self-service capabilities: Can developers provision what they need without waiting for other teams?

Good documentation: Can new team members become productive quickly? Are common tasks well-documented?

Notice what's not on this list: specific technology choices.

The Platform Engineering Movement

This is why I'm so excited about the platform engineering movement. It's focused on outcomes (developer productivity) rather than technologies (specific tools).

Platform teams ask questions like:

  • How can we reduce the time from code commit to production deployment?
  • How can we make it easier for developers to understand system behavior?
  • How can we eliminate repetitive, manual tasks from developer workflows?
  • How can we make development environments more consistent and reliable?

These are the right questions, regardless of whether you're using Kubernetes or VMs, microservices or monoliths.

The Configuration Management Revolution

One area where I think we're on the cusp of a real breakthrough is configuration management. The traditional approach of environment-specific config files is clearly broken at scale.

The teams that are solving this problem aren't necessarily using the newest tools - they're using whatever tools let them separate application configuration from infrastructure configuration effectively.

Some are using sophisticated GitOps workflows with Kubernetes. Others are using simple templating systems with traditional deployment tools. What matters isn't the implementation - it's the separation of concerns.

Industry Reality Check

Our latest analysis of cloud-native conversations shows some interesting trends:

  • Kubernetes adoption is accelerating, but so are complaints about complexity
  • Teams are increasingly looking for managed services rather than self-hosting
  • The most successful Kubernetes implementations focus on developer experience, not infrastructure purity
  • GitOps is becoming popular not because it's technically superior, but because it provides better audit trails and change management

Avoiding the Technology Trap

How do you avoid getting caught up in technology religious wars while still making good technical decisions?

Start with developer interviews: What are the actual pain points in your development workflow? Don't assume - ask.

Measure what matters: Track metrics that correlate with business outcomes - deployment frequency, lead time, recovery time, change failure rate.

Focus on incremental improvement: Make your current systems better before replacing them entirely.

Choose boring technology: Use proven, well-understood tools unless you have a compelling reason to do otherwise.

Design for developer experience: Every technical decision should be evaluated based on how it affects daily developer productivity.

The Humanitec Perspective

At Humanitec, we're deliberately technology-agnostic. Our platform can work with Kubernetes or VMs, with any CI/CD system, with any cloud provider or on-premises infrastructure.

Why? Because we believe the value is in the abstraction layer, not in the underlying technology choices. The goal is to give developers a consistent interface regardless of what's running underneath.

This approach lets teams optimize for developer experience while still having the flexibility to choose the best underlying technologies for their specific constraints and requirements.

The Real Competition

Here's something that might be controversial: our real competition isn't other developer platform companies. It's the status quo of developers spending time on operational concerns instead of building features that serve customers.

Every minute a developer spends wrestling with deployment configurations, debugging environment issues, or waiting for infrastructure to be provisioned is a minute not spent on solving business problems.

That's the real enemy. Not other technology choices, not other vendors - just inefficiency and wasted developer time.

Looking Forward

I think we're moving toward a future where the underlying technology becomes increasingly invisible to developers. Where they can focus on application logic and business requirements while the platform handles all the operational complexity.

This isn't about dumbing down development or hiding important details - it's about creating the right level of abstraction so that developers can focus on what they do best.

The religious wars will continue, but the teams that win will be the ones that stay focused on developer experience regardless of which gods they worship.

What's your experience? Have you seen technology choices improve or hinder developer productivity at your organization?

Best regards,
Kaspar
