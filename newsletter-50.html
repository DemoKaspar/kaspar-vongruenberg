Newsletter Issue #50

Can you beat the crisis or will the crisis beat you?

Hey {NAME},

50 issues! That's quite a milestone. When I started this newsletter, I never imagined I'd have so much to say about platform engineering and developer productivity. But here we are, and the topic just keeps getting richer and more relevant.

Today I want to talk about something that's been weighing on my mind: how economic uncertainty affects platform engineering initiatives, and what you can do to crisis-proof your platform investments.

The Platform Engineering Reality Check

Let's be honest - when budgets get tight, platform engineering often feels like the first thing on the chopping block. It's not directly customer-facing, the ROI can be hard to quantify, and it requires significant upfront investment.

I've been talking to a lot of CTOs and engineering leaders lately, and many are being asked to justify their platform teams and infrastructure investments. Some are being asked to cut platform teams entirely.

But here's the thing - this is exactly when platform engineering becomes most valuable. When you need to do more with less, when you need to ship faster with smaller teams, when you need to reduce operational overhead and focus on core business value - that's when a good platform pays for itself 10x over.

The Crisis-Resistant Platform

Not all platforms are created equal when it comes to economic resilience. The platforms that survive budget cuts have a few key characteristics:

1. Clear, measurable ROI
They can show exactly how much time and money they're saving. Not in abstract metrics, but in hard numbers: "Our platform reduces deployment time from 4 hours to 15 minutes, saving us $50k per month in developer time."

2. High developer adoption
If your platform is used by 80%+ of your engineering teams, it's very hard to justify cutting it. If it's used by 20%, it's an easy target.

3. Operational cost reduction
The best platforms don't just make developers more productive - they reduce operational costs. Automated scaling, better resource utilization, fewer incidents, less manual intervention.

4. Business outcome correlation
They can draw a direct line between platform improvements and business metrics - faster feature delivery, higher uptime, better customer experience.

Building Your Business Case

If you're being asked to justify your platform investments, here's how to frame the conversation:

Start with waste elimination:
How much time do your developers spend on toil? How much do you spend on infrastructure that's over-provisioned because it's manually managed? How much revenue do you lose to downtime that could be prevented by better operational practices?

Focus on velocity multipliers:
A good platform doesn't just make individual tasks faster - it enables entire classes of work that weren't possible before. Self-service infrastructure, automated compliance, instant environment provisioning.

Quantify the compounding effects:
Platform improvements compound over time. A 10% improvement in deployment speed affects every deployment, forever. That's not a one-time saving - it's an investment that keeps paying dividends.

What to Double Down On

If you're facing budget pressure, here's where I'd focus my platform investments:

Developer self-service:
The fastest way to reduce operational costs is to eliminate manual work. Every workflow that requires a human operator is a bottleneck and a cost center.

Resource optimization:
Automated scaling, rightsizing, and resource management can save enormous amounts on cloud bills. I've seen teams reduce their infrastructure costs by 40%+ with good platform automation.

Incident reduction:
Every incident costs money - in engineer time, lost productivity, and sometimes direct business impact. Platforms that prevent incidents through better testing, monitoring, and deployment practices provide immediate ROI.

What You Can Cut

At the same time, be honest about what's nice-to-have versus essential:

Fancy UIs can wait if the underlying automation works well through APIs or CLIs.

Perfect abstractions can be iterated on over time. Start with good-enough abstractions that solve 80% of use cases.

Comprehensive tooling can be built incrementally. Focus on the highest-impact tools first.

The Long Game

Here's what I believe: the companies that continue investing in platform engineering through economic uncertainty will emerge stronger on the other side. They'll have more efficient operations, happier developers, and the ability to scale faster when growth returns.

The companies that cut platform investments to save money in the short term will find themselves with technical debt, operational inefficiencies, and burned-out engineering teams.

Economic downturns are when competitive advantages are built, not when they're abandoned.

What's your experience been? Are you seeing budget pressure on platform initiatives? How are you making the business case?

Thanks for sticking with me for 50 issues. Here's to 50 more!

Kaspar
