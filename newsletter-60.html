Newsletter Issue #60

Reference architectures for enterprise-grade Internal Developer Platforms

Hey {NAME},

Issue #60 - we're really hitting our stride here! Today I want to dive deep into something I get asked about constantly: what does an enterprise-grade Internal Developer Platform actually look like architecturally?

I've had the privilege of working with some incredible platform teams over the years, and I've started to see clear patterns emerge in how the most successful enterprise platforms are structured.

The Enterprise Reality

Before we dive into architectures, let's be honest about what "enterprise-grade" actually means. It's not just about scale (though that matters). Enterprise platforms have to deal with:

  • Regulatory compliance - SOC2, GDPR, HIPAA, industry-specific requirements
  • Legacy integration - mainframes, decades-old databases, proprietary systems
  • Organizational complexity - multiple business units, acquisitions, distributed teams
  • Risk aversion - change management processes, security reviews, audit trails
  • Multi-cloud requirements - often driven by acquisitions or vendor diversification

These constraints fundamentally shape platform architecture in ways that startup platforms don't have to consider.

The Layered Architecture Pattern

The most successful enterprise platforms I've seen follow a layered architecture pattern:

Layer 1: Infrastructure Abstraction
This layer abstracts cloud providers, on-premises infrastructure, and legacy systems. It provides a consistent interface regardless of where workloads actually run.

Layer 2: Resource Management
This layer handles provisioning, scaling, and lifecycle management of infrastructure resources. It implements policy, governance, and cost management.

Layer 3: Platform Services
This layer provides higher-level services that applications need - databases, messaging, caching, secrets management, etc. These are often wrappers around managed services.

Layer 4: Developer Interface
This layer provides the interfaces that developers actually interact with - CLIs, web UIs, APIs, GitOps workflows, etc.

Reference Architecture #1: The Federated Platform

This pattern works well for large enterprises with multiple business units:

Central platform team provides the foundational layers - infrastructure abstraction, core security, networking, monitoring.

Business unit platform teams build on top of the central foundation to provide domain-specific capabilities and developer interfaces.

Advantages:

  • Allows business units to move at their own pace
  • Enables domain-specific optimizations
  • Distributes platform development effort
  • Reduces bottlenecks on central team

Challenges:

  • Risk of fragmentation and inconsistency
  • Complex governance requirements
  • Difficult to maintain security standards
  • Higher overall platform development cost

Reference Architecture #2: The Centralized Platform

This pattern works well for enterprises with strong central IT and consistent requirements across business units:

Single platform team provides all layers of the platform, from infrastructure to developer interfaces.

Business units are consumers of the platform, with limited ability to customize or extend.

Advantages:

  • Consistent security and compliance
  • Lower overall platform development cost
  • Easier to maintain and evolve
  • Clear governance and policy enforcement

Challenges:

  • Can become a bottleneck for business units
  • May not fit all use cases equally well
  • Risk of becoming too generic
  • Requires strong product management

Reference Architecture #3: The Hybrid Platform

This pattern combines elements of both approaches and is what I see most often in practice:

Central platform team provides core infrastructure, security, and governance capabilities.

Shared platform services are provided centrally but can be extended or customized by business units.

Local platform teams focus on developer experience and domain-specific tooling.

This usually evolves from a centralized platform that gradually enables more customization and extension points as the organization matures.

Key Architectural Decisions

Regardless of which pattern you choose, there are several key architectural decisions that define enterprise platforms:

Multi-tenancy strategy: How do you isolate workloads and data between different teams and business units? This affects everything from networking to database design.

Secrets management: How do you handle secrets and sensitive configuration across multiple environments and compliance domains?

Network architecture: How do you handle connectivity between cloud, on-premises, and legacy systems while maintaining security?

Data persistence: How do you provide database and storage services that meet different compliance, performance, and availability requirements?

Observability: How do you provide unified monitoring, logging, and tracing across heterogeneous infrastructure?

Implementation Patterns

Here are some specific patterns I see working well in enterprise platforms:

GitOps for everything: All platform configuration, policies, and application deployments go through Git. This provides audit trails and change management that enterprises need.

Policy as code: Security policies, compliance rules, and governance controls are implemented as code and enforced automatically.

Service mesh for connectivity: Istio or similar provides consistent security, observability, and traffic management across services.

Operator pattern for stateful services: Kubernetes operators manage databases, message queues, and other stateful services consistently.

Infrastructure as code for everything: All infrastructure is defined declaratively and managed through automated pipelines.

Anti-Patterns to Avoid

Some things I consistently see not work in enterprise platforms:

Trying to build everything yourself: Enterprises have complex requirements, but that doesn't mean you need to build every component from scratch. Use managed services and existing tools where possible.

Ignoring legacy systems: Your platform needs to integrate with existing systems, not replace them all. Design for coexistence.

Over-engineering the abstraction layer: Abstractions should simplify common cases, not hide all complexity. Provide escape hatches for advanced use cases.

Neglecting the migration path: You need a clear strategy for moving existing applications to the platform. Big-bang migrations don't work in enterprises.

Success Metrics

How do you know if your enterprise platform is successful?

  • Adoption rate: What percentage of applications are running on the platform?
  • Time to value: How long does it take for teams to get value from the platform?
  • Operational efficiency: How much operational overhead has been reduced?
  • Compliance posture: How much easier is it to maintain compliance?
  • Developer satisfaction: Are developers happier and more productive?

The best enterprise platforms I've seen can show clear business impact in all of these areas.

Where to Start

If you're building an enterprise platform, start with the infrastructure abstraction layer. Get the basics right - networking, security, monitoring - before you start building higher-level services.

Focus on solving real problems for real teams, not building a theoretical perfect platform. Enterprise platforms succeed through evolution, not revolution.
