Newsletter Issue #70

14 days to your minimum viable platform

Hey {NAME},

Issue #70! We're really hitting our stride. Today I want to talk about something I get asked about constantly: "How quickly can we build a minimum viable platform?"

The answer might surprise you: 14 days. Not 14 months, not 14 weeks - 14 days.

But here's the catch - it's only possible if you're ruthless about scope and smart about what you build versus what you buy/adopt.

The 14-Day Challenge

I've been working with teams to test this hypothesis: what's the minimum platform that provides real value to developers in the shortest possible time?

Not a perfect platform. Not a complete platform. Just something that makes developers' lives measurably better than they were 14 days ago.

Here's what we've learned:

Day 1-2: Assessment and Goal Setting

Day 1: Developer interviews
Talk to 5-10 developers about their biggest pain points. Don't guess, don't assume - ask. Common answers: "Deployments are painful," "Environment setup takes forever," "I spend too much time on configuration."

Day 2: Pick ONE problem
Choose the single biggest pain point that affects the most developers. Resist the urge to solve everything. Your MVP platform should solve one problem really well.

Day 3-5: Architecture and Tool Selection

Day 3: Architecture decisions
Make the big architectural decisions quickly. Cloud provider? Container orchestration? GitOps or push-based deployment? CI/CD platform? Don't overthink it - pick good-enough solutions and move forward.

Day 4-5: Tool selection
Choose tools that are mature, well-documented, and widely adopted. This is not the time for cutting-edge technology. Some safe choices:

  • GitHub Actions or GitLab CI for CI/CD
  • ArgoCD for GitOps deployments
  • Helm for application packaging
  • Prometheus + Grafana for observability
  • cert-manager for TLS certificates

Day 6-10: Core Platform Implementation

Day 6-7: Infrastructure foundation
Set up the basic infrastructure - Kubernetes cluster, networking, storage, basic security. Use managed services wherever possible. Don't build what you can buy.

Day 8-9: CI/CD pipeline
Build a simple but robust CI/CD pipeline. One pipeline template that works for 80% of applications. Optimization can come later.

Day 10: GitOps setup
Set up ArgoCD or Flux for automated deployments. Create a simple application-of-applications pattern for managing multiple services.

Day 11-12: Developer Interface

Day 11: Documentation
Write clear, step-by-step documentation for deploying applications to your platform. Include examples and troubleshooting guides.

Day 12: Developer tooling
Create simple CLI tools or scripts that abstract away platform complexity. A single command to deploy an application, check logs, or scale services.

Day 13-14: Testing and Iteration

Day 13: Internal testing
Deploy a real application to your platform. Fix the most obvious issues and gaps.

Day 14: Developer feedback
Have one of the developers you interviewed on Day 1 try to use your platform. Watch them struggle, take notes, and plan your next iteration.

What You Get After 14 Days

Your MVP platform won't be perfect, but it should provide:

  • Consistent deployments: The same process works for all applications
  • Basic observability: Developers can see if their applications are healthy
  • Self-service: Developers can deploy without involving ops teams
  • GitOps workflow: All changes go through Git for auditability
  • Documentation: Clear instructions for using the platform

More importantly, you'll have a foundation to build on and real user feedback to guide your next steps.

What You Don't Get

Your 14-day platform won't have:

  • Multi-tenancy or advanced RBAC
  • Sophisticated monitoring and alerting
  • Database provisioning or data management
  • Advanced networking or service mesh
  • Comprehensive security scanning
  • Cost management or resource quotas

That's okay! These are all important, but they're not minimum viable. They're maximum viable.

The Secret Sauce

The key to building a platform in 14 days is ruthless prioritization:

Build for the 80% case: Your platform should work perfectly for the most common applications and use cases. Edge cases can wait.

Embrace constraints: Limit technology choices, deployment patterns, and configuration options. Fewer choices mean less complexity.

Use managed services: Don't run your own databases, message queues, or monitoring systems unless you absolutely have to.

Copy successful patterns: Don't reinvent the wheel. Find teams who have solved similar problems and adapt their approaches.

Ship fast, iterate faster: Get something working quickly, then improve it based on real feedback.

Real-World Examples

I've seen several teams successfully deploy 14-day platforms:

E-commerce startup: Built a platform focused on fast, safe deployments. Simple Helm charts + GitHub Actions + ArgoCD. Reduced deployment time from 2 hours to 10 minutes.

Financial services company: Built a platform focused on compliance and auditability. Everything goes through Git, every change is logged, every deployment is reversible.

Media company: Built a platform focused on scaling and cost management. Automatic horizontal scaling, resource limits, and cost visibility.

Each platform solved different problems because each team had different pain points.

Common Pitfalls

Teams that fail the 14-day challenge usually make these mistakes:

Trying to solve too many problems: You can't build a comprehensive platform in 14 days. Pick one problem and solve it well.

Over-engineering: Perfect is the enemy of done. Build something that works, not something that's beautiful.

Ignoring user feedback: Your assumptions about what developers need are probably wrong. Talk to them early and often.

Building instead of buying: There are excellent open-source tools for almost every platform need. Use them.

After the 14 Days

What happens after you have your MVP platform?

Weeks 3-4: Focus on adoption. Get your first real applications deployed. Fix the most critical issues.

Month 2: Add the next most important capability based on user feedback. Maybe it's better monitoring, maybe it's database provisioning, maybe it's multi-environment support.

Month 3+: Continue iterating based on real usage patterns and feedback. Add capabilities as they become limiting factors.

The Long Game

The goal isn't to build a perfect platform in 14 days. The goal is to build something valuable quickly, get it into users' hands, and learn from their experience.

Platform engineering is ultimately about solving real problems for real developers. The faster you can get something working in front of users, the faster you can learn what actually matters.

14 days to value, then iterate forever.

Have you tried building a platform quickly? What worked? What didn't? I'd love to hear about your experiences.
