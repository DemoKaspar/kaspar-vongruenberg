Newsletter Issue #75

What can you learn from Rilke, Graham and Napoleon about platform engineering?

Hey {NAME},

Issue #75! We're getting close to the big 8-0. Today I want to do something a bit different and draw some lessons for platform engineering from three unlikely sources: a poet, a painter, and a general.

Bear with me - there's method to this madness.

Rilke on Patience: "Let everything happen to you"

Rainer Maria Rilke wrote: "Perhaps all the dragons in our lives are princesses who are only waiting to see us act, once, with beauty and courage." He also famously advised a young correspondent to "live the questions" and let things develop naturally.

This resonates deeply with platform engineering. One of the biggest mistakes I see platform teams make is trying to solve every problem on day one. They build comprehensive platforms that address every possible use case, anticipating every future need.

But the best platforms grow organically, in response to actual developer needs rather than anticipated ones. They start small, with clear constraints and focused solutions. They "live the questions" rather than rushing to provide answers.

The Rilke approach to platform engineering:

  • Start with the most common, well-understood use cases
  • Build feedback loops with developers early and often
  • Resist the urge to anticipate every possible requirement
  • Let the platform evolve based on actual usage patterns
  • Embrace the tension between what developers ask for and what they actually need

Your platform doesn't need to be perfect on day one. It needs to be useful and evolvable.

Martha Graham on Process: "All that is important is this one moment"

The great choreographer Martha Graham said: "All that is important is this one moment in movement. Make the moment important, vital, and worth living. Do not let it slip away unnoticed and unused."

In platform engineering, we often get so focused on the grand vision - the perfect developer experience, the complete self-service platform, the fully automated infrastructure - that we lose sight of the immediate moment.

But platforms are built one moment at a time, one developer interaction at a time. Each deployment, each configuration change, each support ticket is an opportunity to make the platform better.

The Graham approach to platform engineering:

  • Focus on making today's deployment experience better than yesterday's
  • Pay attention to the small friction points, not just the big architecture decisions
  • Treat every developer interaction as a chance to gather feedback
  • Iterate quickly on small improvements rather than waiting for big releases
  • Celebrate incremental progress, not just major milestones

The perfect platform is built from a thousand small improvements, not one grand design.

Napoleon on Logistics: "An army marches on its stomach"

Napoleon understood that military strategy is ultimately about logistics. You can have the best battle plans in the world, but if your supply lines fail, you lose.

Platform engineering is logistics for software development. All the brilliant architecture and elegant abstractions in the world don't matter if your CI/CD is flaky, your secrets management is painful, or your monitoring is inadequate.

Yet we often get seduced by the glamorous technical challenges and neglect the mundane operational details that determine whether our platforms actually work in practice.

The Napoleon approach to platform engineering:

  • Invest heavily in the boring, foundational capabilities
  • Make reliability and operational excellence your top priorities
  • Understand that developer productivity depends on logistical efficiency
  • Don't deploy new capabilities without the operational foundation to support them
  • Remember that platforms fail from operational issues more often than architectural ones

Your platform's supply lines - monitoring, alerting, backup and recovery, scaling, security - are more important than its strategic capabilities.

Synthesis: The Art of Platform Engineering

What do these three perspectives have in common? They all recognize that execution matters more than vision, that process matters more than plans, and that small, consistent actions compound into extraordinary results.

Platform engineering is ultimately an art - the art of creating conditions for other people to do their best work. Like all art forms, it requires:

Patience - to let the platform evolve naturally rather than forcing a predetermined vision

Presence - to pay attention to the immediate needs and friction points rather than getting lost in grand architectural visions

Pragmatism - to focus on the operational foundation that makes everything else possible

Practical Applications

How does this translate to your day-to-day platform work?

When planning your roadmap: Start with Rilke's patience. What are the actual problems developers are facing today? What's the smallest useful thing you can build?

When designing interfaces: Channel Graham's focus on the moment. How does this specific interaction feel? What small friction can you eliminate?

When prioritizing work: Think like Napoleon about logistics. What operational capabilities need to be bulletproof before you can build the next layer?

The Long View

Building platforms is a long-term endeavor that requires both strategic vision and tactical execution. But the vision without the execution is just dreaming, and the execution without the vision is just busy work.

The combination of Rilke's patience, Graham's presence, and Napoleon's pragmatism gives us a framework for building platforms that are both visionary and practical, both ambitious and grounded.

What unexpected sources of wisdom have influenced your approach to platform engineering? I'd love to hear about the books, ideas, or people outside of technology that have shaped your thinking.

Sometimes the best insights come from the most unexpected places.

Kaspar
