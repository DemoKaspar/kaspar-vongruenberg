Newsletter Issue #77

Silos are fantastic

Hey {NAME},

Issue #77! Today I want to defend something that's become a dirty word in our industry: silos.

Everyone talks about "breaking down silos" as if silos are inherently bad. But I think we've misunderstood what silos are and why they exist. Good silos are actually essential for building scalable organizations and effective platforms.

The problem isn't silos themselves - it's badly designed silos with poor interfaces.

Why Silos Exist

Silos aren't organizational failures - they're organizational adaptations. They exist because:

Specialization creates efficiency: A team that focuses on databases will be better at database management than a team that does databases, networking, security, and frontend development.

Context switching is expensive: When teams have to understand too many different domains, they spend more time switching contexts than actually building things.

Expertise requires focus: Deep knowledge in any technical domain requires sustained attention and practice. You can't become a Kubernetes expert if you're also trying to master machine learning, database optimization, and security.

Accountability needs boundaries: Clear ownership is impossible without clear boundaries. If everything is everyone's responsibility, nothing is anyone's responsibility.

The Good Silo vs. Bad Silo

The difference between good silos and bad silos isn't whether they exist - it's how well they're designed.

Bad silos:

  • Have unclear interfaces and handoff points
  • Optimize for their own efficiency at the expense of overall flow
  • Communicate primarily through tickets and formal processes
  • Make decisions without understanding downstream impact
  • Hoard information and expertise

Good silos:

  • Have well-defined APIs and service boundaries
  • Optimize for the success of their customers (other teams)
  • Communicate through self-service interfaces and clear documentation
  • Understand and measure their impact on overall system performance
  • Share knowledge and enable others to be successful

Platform Engineering as Silo Design

This is why I think platform engineering is fundamentally about designing good silos. Platform teams create boundaries and interfaces that allow other teams to be more effective.

Think about what a platform team does:

They create abstraction boundaries: Application teams don't need to understand Kubernetes internals because the platform abstracts that complexity away.

They define service interfaces: Instead of application teams manually configuring load balancers, they use platform APIs that handle load balancing automatically.

They enable self-service: Instead of requiring tickets and manual processes, they build tools that let other teams accomplish their goals independently.

They optimize for customer success: Platform teams measure their success based on how productive they make application teams, not how sophisticated their infrastructure is.

The Interface Design Problem

Most organizational problems blamed on "silos" are actually interface design problems.

For example:

The problem: "The security team is a bottleneck. Every change requires a security review that takes two weeks."

The silo-breaking solution: "Let's embed security engineers in every product team."

The interface design solution: "Let's build security policies into the platform so that compliant deployments are automatic and non-compliant deployments are impossible."

The interface design solution preserves the specialized expertise while eliminating the bottleneck.

Platform Interfaces vs. Team Interfaces

One insight I've had recently: the best platform teams don't just build technical interfaces (APIs, CLIs, UIs) - they also design organizational interfaces.

They think about:

  • How should other teams interact with the platform team?
  • When should teams self-serve vs. ask for help?
  • How should feature requests and bug reports flow?
  • How should knowledge and expertise be shared?
  • How should the platform team communicate changes and updates?

Great platform teams design these organizational interfaces as carefully as they design their technical interfaces.

The Conway's Law Opportunity

Conway's Law states that organizations design systems that mirror their communication structures. Most people treat this as a constraint, but I think it's an opportunity.

If you want to build modular, well-architected systems, design modular, well-architected organizations. Create teams with clear boundaries, well-defined interfaces, and strong internal cohesion.

This means:

  • Team boundaries should match system boundaries - don't split ownership of a service across multiple teams
  • Team interfaces should match system interfaces - if two teams need to collaborate closely, their systems should integrate cleanly
  • Team autonomy should match system autonomy - teams should be able to make changes to their systems without coordinating with other teams

The Self-Service Test

Here's a simple test for whether your silos are well-designed: how much self-service do they enable?

Good silos make it easy for their customers (other teams) to accomplish their goals without direct interaction. Bad silos require constant coordination and communication.

Examples of good silo interfaces:

  • Platform team: Provides APIs for deploying applications, provisioning databases, and accessing logs
  • Security team: Builds security scanning into CI/CD pipelines and provides compliant-by-default platform configurations
  • Data team: Provides self-service analytics tools and data pipelines that product teams can use independently

Microservices and Micro-teams

This is also why the microservices pattern works well with small, focused teams. Each service is owned by a team that can make changes independently, deploy on their own schedule, and optimize for their specific use case.

The key insight: the "micro" in microservices isn't just about service size - it's about team size and team autonomy.

A good microservice is owned by a team small enough to understand the entire service deeply, with interfaces clean enough that other teams can use it without understanding its internals.

When Silos Go Wrong

Silos become problematic when:

Boundaries are in the wrong places: Splitting related functionality across teams creates coordination overhead and interface complexity.

Interfaces are poorly designed: Complex, poorly documented interfaces make it hard for other teams to be successful.

Teams optimize locally: When teams optimize for their own metrics instead of overall system performance, silos become competitive instead of collaborative.

Knowledge isn't shared: When expertise is hoarded within silos, the organization becomes fragile and can't adapt to change.

Building Better Silos

If you want to build effective silos in your organization:

Design boundaries thoughtfully: Team boundaries should minimize coordination requirements and maximize team autonomy.

Invest in interface design: Good APIs, clear documentation, and self-service tools are force multipliers for organizational efficiency.

Measure customer success: Teams should be measured on how well they enable their customers (other teams) to be successful.

Share knowledge actively: Silos should develop expertise, but they shouldn't hoard it. Build systems for sharing knowledge across boundaries.

Design for evolution: Silos and interfaces should be designed to evolve as the organization and technology change.

The Platform Team as Interface Designer

This is why I think the platform team's most important role isn't building infrastructure - it's designing interfaces that enable other teams to be successful.

The best platform teams think like API designers:

  • How can we make common tasks simple?
  • How can we provide sensible defaults while allowing customization?
  • How can we evolve our interfaces without breaking existing users?
  • How can we make our interfaces discoverable and learnable?
  • How can we gather feedback and improve based on actual usage?

The Paradox

Here's the paradox: the best way to break down bad silos is to build good silos. Well-designed boundaries and interfaces eliminate the coordination overhead and communication problems that make silos feel like barriers.

Instead of trying to eliminate specialization and boundaries, we should focus on designing better boundaries and interfaces.

What's your experience with silos? Have you seen examples of well-designed boundaries that enable autonomy and efficiency?

Kaspar
