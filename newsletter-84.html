Newsletter Issue #84

How to measure platform success beyond uptime

Hey {NAME},

"Our platform is 99.9% available, but developers are still complaining."

This statement captures a fundamental problem in platform engineering: traditional infrastructure metrics don't tell you if your platform is actually successful.

Uptime is necessary but not sufficient. Here's what to measure instead.

The Problem with Traditional Metrics

Most platform teams measure their success like infrastructure teams:

  • System uptime and availability
  • Response times and throughput
  • Resource utilization and costs
  • Error rates and MTTR

These metrics matter, but they don't answer the most important question: Are developers more productive because of your platform?

A platform can be technically perfect but still fail if developers can't use it effectively.

The Three Layers of Platform Success Metrics

Layer 1: Platform Health Metrics

Basic technical health—your platform needs to work before it can be useful.

  • Availability: Platform uptime and service availability
  • Performance: API response times, deployment speeds, build times
  • Reliability: Error rates, incident frequency, MTTR
  • Scalability: Resource utilization, capacity headroom

Think of these as hygiene factors—they don't create platform success, but their absence prevents it.

Layer 2: Developer Experience Metrics

How effectively can developers use your platform to accomplish their goals?

  • Time to first deployment: How long does it take a new developer to deploy their first application?
  • Developer onboarding time: Time from platform access to productive usage
  • Self-service success rate: Percentage of developer requests handled without human intervention
  • Platform adoption rate: Percentage of eligible teams/applications using the platform
  • Feature utilization: Which platform features are actually being used?
  • Developer satisfaction: NPS scores, satisfaction surveys, qualitative feedback

Layer 3: Business Impact Metrics

How does your platform contribute to organizational goals?

  • Developer productivity: Deployment frequency, lead time, cycle time
  • Quality improvements: Reduced defect rates, faster time-to-resolution
  • Cost efficiency: Infrastructure cost per application, developer cost per feature
  • Risk reduction: Security compliance rates, incident prevention
  • Innovation enablement: New product/feature launch speed

Measuring Developer Productivity

The DORA metrics are a great starting point for measuring platform impact on productivity:

Deployment Frequency

  • How often are teams deploying to production?
  • Is this increasing over time as teams adopt platform capabilities?
  • Are there differences between teams using and not using the platform?

Lead Time for Changes

  • How long from code commit to production deployment?
  • Which parts of the pipeline add the most time?
  • How has this changed as platform features mature?

Change Failure Rate

  • What percentage of deployments cause production failures?
  • Are platform-assisted deployments more reliable?
  • How do platform guardrails affect failure rates?

Time to Recovery

  • How quickly can teams detect and resolve production issues?
  • Do platform observability tools reduce MTTR?
  • How do platform rollback capabilities affect recovery time?

Platform-Specific Success Metrics

Golden Path Metrics

  • Golden path adoption: Percentage of teams using standard workflows
  • Golden path success rate: How often do standard workflows succeed?
  • Golden path completion time: How long do common tasks take?
  • Golden path satisfaction: Developer feedback on standard workflows

Self-Service Metrics

  • API usage patterns: Which APIs are used most frequently?
  • Portal usage: What do developers do in your self-service interfaces?
  • Documentation effectiveness: Are developers finding answers in your docs?
  • Support ticket reduction: Are self-service capabilities reducing support load?

Platform Stickiness

  • Retention rate: Do teams continue using the platform over time?
  • Feature expansion: Do teams adopt additional platform features?
  • Voluntary migrations: Are teams choosing to migrate to the platform?
  • Platform advocacy: Are teams recommending the platform to others?

Measuring Developer Satisfaction

Quantitative metrics tell you what's happening. Qualitative feedback tells you why.

Regular satisfaction surveys

  • Quarterly NPS surveys with follow-up questions
  • Feature-specific feedback after releases
  • Annual comprehensive developer experience surveys

Structured user research

  • User interviews with developers from different teams
  • Usability testing for new platform features
  • Journey mapping for key developer workflows

Feedback channel analysis

  • Support ticket sentiment analysis
  • Slack/forum discussion themes
  • Feature request patterns and priorities

Creating Your Platform Success Dashboard

Your dashboard should tell a story about platform value:

Executive Summary (top level)

  • Platform adoption rate
  • Developer productivity trend (DORA metrics)
  • Developer satisfaction score
  • Business impact summary

Platform Health (second level)

  • System availability and performance
  • Platform capacity and scaling trends
  • Incident frequency and resolution time

Developer Experience (third level)

  • Onboarding and time-to-value metrics
  • Self-service success and usage patterns
  • Support volume and resolution trends
  • Feature adoption and utilization

Avoiding Metric Pitfalls

Vanity metrics

Metrics that look good but don't drive decisions. Examples: total API calls, number of users registered, lines of documentation written.

Lagging indicators only

Measuring outcomes without understanding leading indicators. Include metrics that predict future success or failure.

Too many metrics

Tracking everything creates noise, not insight. Focus on the metrics that drive platform improvement decisions.

Metrics without context

Numbers without explanation don't drive action. Always include context about what the metrics mean and why they matter.

Using Metrics to Drive Platform Evolution

The goal of measurement isn't reporting—it's improvement:

Regular metric reviews

  • Weekly platform health reviews
  • Monthly developer experience analysis
  • Quarterly business impact assessment
  • Annual platform success retrospective

Data-driven roadmap decisions

  • Prioritize features based on adoption and satisfaction data
  • Identify friction points from developer journey metrics
  • Validate improvement hypotheses with before/after measurements

Continuous feedback loops

  • Share metrics with developer teams regularly
  • Use metrics to guide platform team retrospectives
  • Correlate platform changes with metric improvements

The Bottom Line

Platform success isn't just about keeping systems running—it's about enabling developer productivity and business outcomes.

Measure technical health to ensure your platform works. Measure developer experience to ensure your platform is usable. Measure business impact to ensure your platform creates value.

All three matter. All three need attention.

But remember: you build platforms for developers, not metrics. The numbers should guide you toward better developer experiences, not become goals in themselves.
