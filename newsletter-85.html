Newsletter Issue #85

Platform team communication patterns that actually work

Hey {NAME},

Platform teams have a communication problem.

You're building infrastructure that affects every developer in your organization, but most of those developers don't understand what you do or why it matters. Meanwhile, you're constantly interrupted by support requests, questions, and "quick asks" that prevent you from doing strategic work.

Sound familiar?

Here are the communication patterns I've seen work for high-performing platform teams.

The Platform Communication Challenge

Platform teams face unique communication challenges:

  • Scale: You serve dozens or hundreds of developers across multiple teams
  • Complexity: Your work involves technical concepts that aren't familiar to most developers
  • Urgency mismatch: Your strategic work often feels less urgent than immediate developer requests
  • Visibility: Platform improvements are often invisible to end users
  • Context switching: You're constantly pulled from deep work to answer questions

Communication Pattern #1: Structured Office Hours

Instead of being available 24/7 for questions, create structured office hours:

How it works:

  • 2-3 scheduled blocks per week (e.g., Tuesday 2-3pm, Thursday 10-11am)
  • Dedicated Slack channel or video room
  • Platform team members rotate responsibility
  • Questions answered in order of arrival
  • Complex issues get scheduled for separate deep-dive sessions

Benefits:

  • Developers know when they can get help
  • Platform team gets uninterrupted work time
  • Common questions become visible to everyone
  • Creates opportunity for group learning

Pro tip:

Record office hours sessions and maintain an FAQ based on common questions.

Communication Pattern #2: Proactive Roadmap Communication

Don't wait for people to ask about your roadmap—actively communicate what you're working on and why.

The platform newsletter:

  • Bi-weekly or monthly email to all developers
  • What shipped recently (with developer benefit explanations)
  • What's coming next (with timelines and impact)
  • Tips and tricks for using platform features
  • Spotlight on teams doing interesting things with the platform

Quarterly platform all-hands:

  • 30-45 minute presentation to all engineering
  • Platform roadmap and strategy
  • Success metrics and adoption numbers
  • Demo of recent improvements
  • Q&A session

Platform changelog:

  • Public log of all platform changes
  • Developer-focused descriptions of what changed
  • Migration guides for breaking changes
  • RSS feed or Slack integration for notifications

Communication Pattern #3: Embedded Success Stories

Instead of talking about platform features, talk about developer success enabled by those features.

Success story format:

  • Problem: What challenge was a team facing?
  • Solution: How did they use the platform to solve it?
  • Impact: What improved for the team?
  • Lessons: What can other teams learn?

Distribution channels:

  • Engineering blog posts
  • All-hands presentation slots
  • Slack channel highlights
  • Team retrospective sharing

Example:

Team Success: Mobile Team Reduces Deployment Time by 80%

Problem: The mobile team was spending 4 hours per deployment managing infrastructure and coordination.

Solution: They adopted our new automated deployment pipeline with built-in testing and rollback capabilities.

Impact: Deployment time dropped from 4 hours to 45 minutes. They now deploy 3x more frequently and have had zero failed deployments this quarter.

Lessons: Teams can reduce deployment overhead by using platform golden paths instead of custom scripts.

Communication Pattern #4: Escalation and Triage System

Create clear paths for different types of platform interactions:

Level 1: Self-service

  • Comprehensive documentation with examples
  • Interactive tutorials and onboarding guides
  • Community Slack channel for peer help
  • Automated troubleshooting tools

Level 2: Structured support

  • Support ticket system with SLAs
  • Office hours for questions and guidance
  • Scheduled consultation sessions for complex topics

Level 3: Direct engagement

  • Dedicated Slack channel for urgent issues
  • On-call rotation for platform emergencies
  • Escalation to platform team lead for strategic decisions

Communication Pattern #5: Developer Advocacy

Designate platform team members as developer advocates who focus on external communication:

Developer advocate responsibilities:

  • Regular one-on-ones with development team leads
  • Gathering and analyzing platform feedback
  • Creating and maintaining documentation
  • Running training sessions and workshops
  • Representing developer needs in platform planning

Rotation model:

Rotate the developer advocate role among platform team members (quarterly or semi-annually) to:

  • Prevent burnout
  • Share knowledge across the team
  • Bring fresh perspectives to developer interactions
  • Develop communication skills across the team

Communication Pattern #6: Metrics-Driven Conversations

Use data to drive productive conversations about platform value and priorities.

Developer productivity metrics:

  • Deployment frequency and success rates
  • Time from commit to production
  • Developer onboarding time
  • Platform adoption rates by team

Platform health metrics:

  • Platform uptime and performance
  • Support ticket volume and resolution time
  • Feature usage and adoption rates
  • Developer satisfaction scores

Business impact metrics:

  • Infrastructure cost per developer
  • Security compliance rates
  • Incident reduction trends
  • Feature delivery velocity

Share these metrics regularly in newsletters, all-hands presentations, and leadership reports.

Communication Anti-Patterns to Avoid

The expert curse

Using technical jargon that your audience doesn't understand. Always explain platform concepts in terms of developer workflows and business outcomes.

The always-on support model

Being available for questions at all times. This creates interruption-driven work and prevents strategic focus.

The feature announcement

Announcing new features without explaining why developers should care or how to use them effectively.

The defensive response

Getting defensive when developers provide critical feedback about the platform. Treat criticism as valuable user research.

The one-way communication

Only broadcasting information without creating channels for questions, feedback, and discussion.

Measuring Communication Effectiveness

How do you know if your communication is working?

Leading indicators:

  • Reduced repetitive questions in support channels
  • Increased self-service usage
  • More constructive feedback and feature requests
  • Higher attendance at platform events

Lagging indicators:

  • Higher platform adoption rates
  • Improved developer satisfaction scores
  • Reduced escalations to platform team members
  • More voluntary participation in platform beta programs

Getting Started

Don't try to implement all these patterns at once. Start with:

  1. Office hours: Create structured time for questions and support
  2. Regular updates: Start a simple monthly newsletter or Slack update
  3. Success stories: Highlight one team's platform success each month
  4. Feedback channels: Create clear ways for developers to provide input

Then expand based on what works for your organization and culture.

The Goal: Partnership, Not Service

Good platform communication creates partnership between platform teams and developers. You're not just a service provider—you're collaborators working toward shared goals.

When communication works well, developers see the platform team as trusted partners who help them ship better software faster.

That's the relationship worth building.
