Newsletter Issue #87

How to build developer empathy in platform teams

Hey {NAME},

The best platform teams I know have one thing in common: deep empathy for the developers they serve.

But here's the challenge—platform engineers often come from infrastructure backgrounds. They think in terms of systems, scalability, and reliability. Developers think in terms of features, deadlines, and user value.

This empathy gap creates platforms that work technically but fail to address developer pain points.

Here's how to bridge that gap.

The Empathy Problem

Common signs that your platform team lacks developer empathy:

  • Developers complain that the platform is "too complex" while your team thinks it's "appropriately sophisticated"
  • You build features no one uses because you solved the wrong problem
  • Support tickets focus on "how do I..." instead of bug reports
  • Developers build workarounds instead of using platform features
  • Your platform adoption metrics are disappointing

Building Developer Empathy

1. Embed with development teams

The most powerful empathy builder is direct experience. Have platform engineers spend time embedded with application teams:

  • **Sprint embeddings:** Platform engineer joins a dev team for 2-3 sprints
  • **Pair programming sessions:** Work together on real features using your platform
  • **On-call rotations:** Platform engineers take turns being on-call for application teams
  • **Code reviews:** Platform engineers participate in application code reviews

This isn't about platform engineers writing application code—it's about understanding the developer workflow and seeing where your platform creates friction.

2. Shadow developer workflows

Watch developers actually use your platform:

  • **New developer onboarding:** Sit with new hires as they set up their first deployment
  • **Feature development sessions:** Watch teams build and deploy features end-to-end
  • **Debugging sessions:** Observe how developers troubleshoot issues with your platform
  • **Emergency response:** See how your platform performs during incidents

Take notes on every friction point, confusion moment, and workaround.

3. Regular user research

Systematic research to understand developer needs:

  • **Quarterly developer surveys:** What's working? What's frustrating? What's missing?
  • **User interviews:** Deep dives with developers from different teams and experience levels
  • **Journey mapping:** Map the complete developer experience for key workflows
  • **Jobs-to-be-done analysis:** Understand what developers are really trying to accomplish

4. Create developer personas

Build detailed personas for your different user types:

**"Sarah the Senior Developer"**

  • 5+ years experience, knows infrastructure well
  • Values control and visibility
  • Frustrated when platforms make decisions she disagrees with
  • Wants APIs and CLI tools, not just web interfaces

**"Jake the Junior Developer"**

  • 1-2 years experience, focused on feature development
  • Wants simple, reliable workflows
  • Gets overwhelmed by too many options
  • Needs clear documentation and examples

**"Maria the Team Lead"**

  • Responsible for team velocity and quality
  • Needs visibility into team's platform usage
  • Concerned about platform adoption across team members
  • Wants metrics and reporting capabilities

Empathy-Driven Platform Design

Start with user stories, not technical requirements

Before building any platform feature, write user stories:

**Instead of:** "We need to implement container orchestration"

**Write:** "As a developer, I want to deploy my application without thinking about servers so I can focus on business logic"

Design workflows, not just tools

Think about complete developer workflows:

  • How does a developer go from idea to deployed feature?
  • What happens when something breaks in production?
  • How does a team onboard a new developer?
  • How do developers manage different environments?

Optimize for developer success, not platform elegance

Sometimes the "right" technical solution creates a poor developer experience. Choose developer experience over technical purity:

  • **Provide sensible defaults** instead of requiring configuration
  • **Hide complexity** behind simple interfaces
  • **Optimize for common cases** even if edge cases become harder
  • **Prioritize reliability** over feature richness

Communication That Shows Empathy

Use developer language

Talk about business outcomes, not technical features:

**Instead of:** "We've implemented a service mesh with advanced traffic management"

**Say:** "You can now deploy features safely with automatic rollback if anything goes wrong"

Show impact on developer workflows

When announcing platform changes, explain the developer impact:

  • What workflows does this improve?
  • What pain points does this solve?
  • What do developers need to do differently?
  • How does this help teams ship faster or more reliably?

Share developer success stories

Highlight how teams are succeeding with your platform:

  • "Team X reduced their deployment time from 2 hours to 10 minutes"
  • "Team Y was able to focus on features instead of infrastructure management"
  • "Team Z eliminated weekend deployments and reduced stress"

Measuring Empathy

How do you know if you're building empathy successfully?

Developer satisfaction metrics

  • Platform NPS scores
  • Developer satisfaction survey results
  • Support ticket sentiment analysis
  • Voluntary platform adoption rates

Usage pattern analysis

  • Are developers using platform features as intended?
  • What workarounds are developers building?
  • Where do developers get stuck in platform workflows?
  • How long does it take developers to accomplish common tasks?

Feedback quality

  • Are developers providing constructive feedback instead of complaints?
  • Are feature requests aligned with actual developer pain points?
  • Do developers trust the platform team with their challenges?

Organizational Support for Developer Empathy

Hire for empathy

When hiring platform engineers, look for:

  • Previous application development experience
  • Strong communication and listening skills
  • Curiosity about user experience and workflow optimization
  • History of working closely with internal customers

Create empathy incentives

  • Include developer satisfaction scores in platform team performance reviews
  • Recognize platform engineers who improve developer workflows
  • Allocate dedicated time for user research and empathy building
  • Make platform adoption metrics visible to the platform team

Establish feedback loops

  • Regular office hours where developers can ask questions
  • Platform team participation in engineering all-hands
  • Developer representatives in platform planning meetings
  • Cross-team retrospectives including platform and development teams

Common Empathy Killers

Assuming developers want what you want

Platform engineers often value technical elegance, performance, and control. Developers often value simplicity, reliability, and speed.

Building for expert users only

Designing for your most sophisticated users while ignoring the majority who want simple, reliable workflows.

Dismissing "simple" requests

Requests for better documentation, clearer error messages, and simpler workflows often get deprioritized for "more important" technical work.

Focusing on technical metrics over user experience

Optimizing for system performance while ignoring developer productivity and satisfaction.

The Long-Term Payoff

Building developer empathy takes time and effort. But the payoff is enormous:

  • Higher platform adoption rates
  • Fewer support tickets and complaints
  • Better feature prioritization decisions
  • Stronger partnership between platform and development teams
  • More satisfied developers and platform engineers

The best platform teams don't just build infrastructure—they solve developer problems.

And you can't solve problems you don't understand.
