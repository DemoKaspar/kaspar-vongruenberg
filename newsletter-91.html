Newsletter Issue #91

Platform economics: build vs. buy decisions

Hey {NAME},

"Should we build our own platform or use an existing solution?"

This question comes up in every platform engineering conversation. The answer isn't obvious, and getting it wrong is expensive.

Here's how to think about platform build vs. buy decisions.

The True Cost of Building

Building your own platform seems cheaper at first. "We have smart engineers, how hard can it be?"

But the real costs of building are often hidden:

Development costs

  • Initial development: 2-3 engineers for 6-12 months minimum
  • Integration work: Connecting with your existing tools and systems
  • Security review: Making sure your platform meets security standards
  • Testing and debugging: Platform bugs affect everyone

Ongoing maintenance costs

  • Bug fixes and security patches
  • Feature development and improvements
  • Documentation and training materials
  • User support and troubleshooting
  • Keeping up with underlying technology changes

Opportunity costs

  • Engineers building platform tools instead of business features
  • Delayed time-to-market for platform capabilities
  • Technical debt from rushing to build platform features

The True Cost of Buying

Commercial platform solutions have obvious costs (licenses, support contracts) but also hidden ones:

Direct costs

  • Software licenses (often per-user or per-workload)
  • Support and maintenance contracts
  • Training and certification for your team
  • Integration and customization work

Indirect costs

  • Vendor lock-in risks
  • Limited customization capabilities
  • Dependency on vendor roadmap and priorities
  • Migration costs if you need to switch vendors

When to Build

Building makes sense when:

You have unique requirements

Your use case is so specific that no existing solution fits. This is rare but does happen, especially in highly regulated industries or companies with unusual technical constraints.

Platform development is your competitive advantage

If you're a cloud provider or tooling company, platform technology might be part of your product strategy.

You have significant platform engineering expertise

Building platforms requires deep expertise in infrastructure, distributed systems, and developer experience. If you don't have this expertise in-house, buying is usually better.

Existing solutions don't integrate with your ecosystem

Sometimes the integration costs of buying exceed the development costs of building.

When to Buy

Buying makes sense when:

Your requirements fit existing solutions

If 80% of your needs match an existing platform, it's usually better to adapt your workflows than build from scratch.

You want to move fast

Buying gets you capabilities immediately. Building takes months or years.

Platform engineering isn't your core competency

Most companies are not platform companies. Focus your engineering talent on your actual business problems.

You have limited engineering resources

Platform engineering requires dedicated team members. If you can't commit 2-3 engineers full-time, you probably shouldn't build.

The Hybrid Approach

Often, the best solution is hybrid:

Buy the foundation, build the differentiation

Use commercial solutions for commodity functionality (compute, storage, basic CI/CD) and build custom tools for your specific workflows.

Start with buying, evolve to building

Use commercial tools to understand your requirements, then build targeted solutions for your most important use cases.

Buy and integrate

Use multiple best-of-breed tools and build integration layers to create a cohesive platform experience.

Evaluation Framework

Here's how I recommend evaluating build vs. buy decisions:

1. Define your requirements clearly

  • What are your must-have features?
  • What are your nice-to-have features?
  • What are your non-functional requirements (scalability, security, compliance)?
  • How will your requirements change over time?

2. Assess your capabilities honestly

  • Do you have experienced platform engineers?
  • Can you commit dedicated resources to platform development?
  • Do you have the appetite for long-term maintenance?

3. Calculate total cost of ownership

  • Include development, maintenance, and opportunity costs for building
  • Include licenses, integration, and switching costs for buying
  • Use a 3-5 year time horizon

4. Consider timing and risk

  • How quickly do you need platform capabilities?
  • What's the risk of building something that doesn't work?
  • What's the risk of vendor dependency?

Common Decision-Making Mistakes

The "not invented here" syndrome

Assuming that building is always better because you understand your requirements better than any vendor.

Underestimating build costs

Focusing only on initial development costs and ignoring long-term maintenance.

Overestimating vendor costs

Comparing vendor prices to developer salaries without including all the hidden costs of building.

All-or-nothing thinking

Assuming you have to either build everything or buy everything.

A Practical Approach

For most organizations, I recommend this approach:

  1. Start by buying or using open-source solutions to understand your needs
  2. Identify gaps where existing solutions don't meet your requirements
  3. Build targeted solutions for your most critical gaps
  4. Integrate everything into a cohesive developer experience
  5. Regularly re-evaluate as your needs and the vendor landscape evolve

The Bottom Line

There's no universal answer to build vs. buy. The right choice depends on your specific context, capabilities, and constraints.

But in general: buy commodity capabilities, build differentiation.

Most companies are not platform companies, and that's okay. Focus your engineering talent on what makes your business unique.
