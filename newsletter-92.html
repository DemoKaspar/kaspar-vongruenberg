Newsletter Issue #92

The platform team burnout problem

Hey {NAME},

Last week, three different platform engineers reached out to me with variations of the same message:

"I love building platforms, but I'm exhausted. We're constantly firefighting, and I never have time to work on the strategic stuff."

Platform team burnout is real, and it's becoming a critical problem in our industry. Let's talk about why it happens and what to do about it.

Why Platform Teams Burn Out

They become the "everything" team

Platform teams often become the dumping ground for any infrastructure work that doesn't fit elsewhere. CI/CD, monitoring, security, compliance, cost optimization—if it touches multiple teams, it becomes the platform team's job.

They're always on call

When the platform breaks, everything breaks. Platform engineers carry the weight of knowing that their mistakes affect every developer in the organization.

They have no clear boundaries

Unlike product teams that can say "that's not our product," platform teams struggle to say no to requests that seem infrastructure-related.

They're constantly context switching

One minute they're debugging a Kubernetes issue, the next they're helping someone with their CI pipeline, then they're in a meeting about compliance requirements.

They get all the blame, none of the credit

When the platform works, developers ship features and get praised. When it breaks, the platform team gets blamed.

The Hidden Costs of Platform Team Burnout

Burned-out platform teams create organizational risks:

  • Knowledge silos: Exhausted engineers don't document or share knowledge
  • Technical debt: No time for proper architecture or refactoring
  • Poor developer experience: Frustrated platform engineers build tools that work for them, not their users
  • Talent attrition: Good platform engineers leave for less stressful roles
  • Innovation stagnation: All time goes to keeping the lights on

Preventing Platform Team Burnout

Define clear boundaries

Create a clear charter for what your platform team does and doesn't do:

  • We do: Build and maintain the core platform infrastructure, golden paths, and developer self-service tools
  • We don't do: Debug application-specific issues, manage individual team's infrastructure, or build custom solutions for single teams

Implement the 70/20/10 rule

  • 70% platform development: Building and improving core platform capabilities
  • 20% support and maintenance: Helping users and fixing issues
  • 10% innovation time: Exploring new technologies and approaches

If your team is spending more than 30% of their time on support and firefighting, you have a problem.

Build self-service capabilities

Every support request your team handles manually is time not spent on strategic work. Invest heavily in:

  • Comprehensive documentation
  • Self-service troubleshooting tools
  • Automated remediation for common issues
  • Clear escalation paths for complex problems

Practice "sustainable on-call"

  • Rotate on-call duties fairly across the team
  • Have clear escalation procedures
  • Post-incident reviews focus on preventing future pages, not blame
  • Give engineers time off after major incidents
  • Set expectations: not every platform issue requires immediate response

Organizational Support for Platform Teams

Leadership backing for saying "no"

Platform teams need organizational support to maintain boundaries. Leadership should back platform teams when they decline requests that fall outside their charter.

Dedicated platform product managers

Platform teams need someone whose job it is to:

  • Prioritize platform features and improvements
  • Communicate platform roadmaps to stakeholders
  • Shield engineers from non-essential meetings and requests
  • Advocate for platform team needs with leadership

Realistic expectations about platform development

Platforms take time to build. Leaders need to understand that:

  • Good platforms require significant upfront investment
  • Platform improvements may not show immediate business impact
  • Technical debt in platforms affects everyone
  • Platform teams need dedicated time for strategic work

Team Structure Solutions

Split platform teams by domain

Instead of one mega platform team, consider:

  • Infrastructure platform team: Core compute, storage, networking
  • Developer experience team: CI/CD, testing, deployment tools
  • Observability team: Monitoring, logging, alerting
  • Security platform team: Security tools and compliance

Embed platform engineers with application teams

Temporarily embed platform engineers with high-need application teams instead of having all platform work go through the central team.

Create platform "office hours"

Instead of constant interruptions, have designated times when platform engineers are available for questions and support.

Individual Strategies for Platform Engineers

If you're a platform engineer dealing with burnout:

  • Advocate for boundaries: Work with your manager to define clear scope for your team
  • Invest in automation: Every manual task you automate is future stress you won't have
  • Document everything: Reduce the bus factor and enable self-service
  • Communicate proactively: Share your roadmap, your constraints, and your wins
  • Take care of yourself: Platform work is a marathon, not a sprint

The Long-Term View

Platform team burnout is often a symptom of success—your platform is valuable enough that everyone wants your help. But sustainable success requires sustainable teams.

Organizations that invest in preventing platform team burnout get better platforms, happier engineers, and more innovation.

The alternative is a cycle of talented platform engineers leaving for less stressful roles, taking their institutional knowledge with them.

Take care of your platform teams. They're taking care of everyone else.
