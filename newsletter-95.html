Newsletter Issue #95

The hidden costs of platform complexity

Hey {NAME},

I was talking to a platform engineer yesterday who said something that stuck with me:

"Our platform does everything we wanted it to do. The problem is, now nobody understands how it works."

This is the hidden cost of platform complexity. You build a beautiful, feature-rich platform that solves every edge case and supports every use case. But in doing so, you've created something that's impossible to operate, debug, or extend.

How Platforms Become Too Complex

Feature creep

Every team has a slightly different need. Instead of saying "that's not our use case," you add another configuration option. Repeat this 100 times and you have a platform that can do everything but is impossible to understand.

Abstraction layers upon abstraction layers

You abstract Kubernetes with Helm charts, then abstract Helm with a custom CLI, then abstract the CLI with a web interface. Each layer hides complexity but also makes debugging harder.

The "one more tool" syndrome

There's a new tool that solves a specific problem beautifully. So you integrate it. Then another. Soon you have 47 different tools in your platform stack.

Premature generalization

Instead of building for your current use cases, you try to build for every possible future use case. The result is over-engineered solutions for simple problems.

The Real Cost of Complexity

Complex platforms create hidden costs:

Cognitive overhead

Developers spend more time understanding the platform than using it. New team members take months to become productive.

Debugging nightmares

When something goes wrong, you need to debug across 12 different systems. Simple issues take hours to resolve.

Platform team burnout

Your platform team spends all their time supporting edge cases and fixing integration issues. They have no time for strategic improvements.

Adoption resistance

Teams look at your platform and think "this looks complicated" so they build workarounds instead of adopting it.

Simplicity as a Platform Strategy

The best platforms I've seen prioritize simplicity over features. Here's how:

The 80/20 rule for platform features

Build for the 80% use case. Let the 20% edge cases find workarounds or use escape hatches. Don't compromise the 80% for the 20%.

Prefer composition over configuration

Instead of building one tool with 100 configuration options, build 5 simple tools that work well together.

Ruthless deprecation

Regularly remove features that aren't being used. Every line of code in your platform has a maintenance cost.

Clear abstraction boundaries

Each layer in your platform should have a clear, well-defined interface. Avoid "leaky abstractions" that expose underlying complexity.

Practical Simplicity Guidelines

The "explain it to a junior developer" test

If you can't explain how your platform works to a junior developer in 10 minutes, it's probably too complex.

The "single point of failure" rule

Any part of your platform should be explainable by one person. If it takes your whole team to understand a component, simplify it.

The "5-minute deployment" goal

New users should be able to deploy something successful within 5 minutes of getting platform access. If it takes longer, your onboarding is too complex.

The "documentation burden" metric

If your platform requires more than 50 pages of documentation to use effectively, you probably need to simplify.

When to Accept Complexity

Sometimes complexity is necessary:

  • When you're solving genuinely complex problems (security, compliance, multi-region deployments)
  • When the complexity is hidden behind simple interfaces
  • When the alternative is every team building their own complex solution

But even then, constantly ask: "Is there a simpler way?"

Simplifying Existing Platforms

If your platform is already too complex:

  1. Audit your features - What percentage of your users actually use each feature?
  2. Map the critical paths - What workflows do 90% of your users follow?
  3. Identify complexity debt - What parts of your platform are hardest to explain/maintain?
  4. Plan the simplification - What can you remove, consolidate, or redesign?
  5. Communicate the changes - Help users understand why simplification helps them too

The Paradox of Platform Design

Simple platforms are harder to build than complex ones. It takes more thought, more discipline, and more courage to say no.

But simple platforms are what teams actually want to use.

Choose simplicity. Your users (and your future self) will thank you.
