Newsletter Issue #98

The platform team's guide to saying "no" (nicely)

Hey {NAME},

Yesterday a platform engineer told me: "I spend more time saying no than building things."

If you're on a platform team, you know this feeling. Every week brings requests that would make your platform slower, less secure, or harder to maintain. But saying "no" to internal customers is an art form.

Here's how to say no without making enemies.

The Wrong Way to Say No

  • "That's not how we do things here"
  • "That would break our architecture"
  • "That's impossible"
  • "We don't have time for that"
  • "That's not our responsibility"

These responses might be technically correct, but they're relationship killers.

The Right Way to Say No

1. Understand the why first

Before you say no to the "what," understand the "why." Often, what teams are asking for isn't really what they need.

Instead of: "We can't give you root access to production"

Try: "Help me understand what you're trying to debug. Let's see if we can solve that together."

2. Explain the trade-offs

Don't just say no—explain what saying yes would cost everyone.

Instead of: "We can't support that database"

Try: "Adding another database to our platform would mean longer maintenance windows for everyone, and we'd need to delay the observability improvements three teams are waiting for. Is there a way to meet your needs with our current stack?"

3. Offer alternatives

Every "no" should come with a "but here's what we can do."

Instead of: "We don't support Windows containers"

Try: "We don't support Windows containers yet, but here are three ways we can help you migrate that workload to Linux."

4. Create an escalation path

Sometimes your "no" should be overruled. Make it easy for teams to escalate when they really need to.

Try: "This doesn't fit our current platform roadmap, but if this is blocking a critical business initiative, let's get your team lead and mine on a call to discuss options."

The Power of "Yes, and..."

Often, you can turn a "no" into a "yes, and" by being creative:

  • Team asks: "Can we have a dedicated database instance?"
  • Platform says: "Yes, and we can set up automated backups, monitoring, and security scanning if you use our managed service instead of running it yourself."
  • Team asks: "Can we use this new deployment tool?"
  • Platform says: "Yes, and if you're willing to be our pilot team, we'll work with you to integrate it into our platform so other teams can benefit too."

When to Say No (and Stick to It)

Some things are non-negotiable:

  • Security violations
  • Compliance requirements
  • Requests that would destabilize the platform for everyone
  • Work that would require you to become a consulting team rather than a platform team

For these, be kind but firm: "I understand this is frustrating, but this is a security requirement we can't compromise on. Let me show you the approved alternatives."

The Long Game

Remember: your goal isn't to make everyone happy. It's to build a platform that serves the organization's long-term needs.

Sometimes that means disappointing individuals to benefit the whole. But how you say no determines whether people see you as a partner or an obstacle.

Say no with empathy, explanation, and alternatives. Your future self (and your platform's adoption rate) will thank you.

Talk soon,

Kaspar
